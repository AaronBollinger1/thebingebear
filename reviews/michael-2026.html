Film Review · 2026

Michael

Biopic Dir. Antoine Fuqua 9 / 10
Listen Along Michael (2026) — Bear Reads the Review
0:00 --:--

This was not intended to be a cinematic spectacle, or a beautiful heartwarming story; while it does both extraordinarily well, they are byproducts of the origin — the man, not by the team. But the team can only tell the tale as true as it fits. Nevertheless, this ends up shocking you to your core when you first take your seat.

The purpose behind this entire production, as it is for many, was the money. We must accept that this is not a film selflessly produced and created by Michael Jackson post-success. This is developed and created by a talented, hungry, established team to shock box office — which, undoubtedly, it has.

As someone who is limited to 20 years of living, 15 of which I can recall somewhat, I did not have the pleasure of understanding Michael Jackson's origins. It turns out, to no retrospective surprise, that he was a spectacle of talent, insecurity, and aura. His music to any human could open one's eyes to the world of soundwaves — but on that matter I digress. This movie does not get the crowd scenes or the stage presence wrong at all. It seems as though Michael is a god amongst men.

The opening stage of his childhood displays Michael in the light of any other human: human. The childhood traumas he experiences are unique to him in their burden of expectation and psyche damage. But this is what pushed him to become the man the masses sing along to. These pivotal scenes where Colman Domingo — acting as Joe Jackson — becomes the audience's least favourite human, were essential in understanding the method behind the madness of Michael.

"Jaafar Jackson is the epitome of everything every movie needs — a star."

Juliano Valdi's moves and lip syncing capture your heart and imagination and beg the question — when does his real music come in, because the voice of him as a child was unparalleled. I am shocked that the Jackson 5 wasn't a global name sooner — but so were the times, with misrepresentation and inequality that blocked this talent, and so many more, from the spotlight. Yet through all of it, he persevered.

I was told someone who reviewed this mentioned a lack of flexibility in the acting. That is the furthest possible statement from the truth. Everyone is confined to a character in a movie — it's how good of that character you are that defines you. Jaafar Jackson doesn't shine alone either, with the whole family tagging along and pulling their weight completely.

What this movie missed most was the production — not just Michael's childhood experiences, but his genius too. I saw glimpses in the studio but can't imagine the work required to write those songs, go through that production process, to reach the end product that was Off the Wall or Thriller. I understand the sticky notes as a fellow member with ADHDish traits, but I just wish there was more foundation to the content the film puts out.

"My favourite parts were the music, the acting, and the dancing — and I cannot express how impressive it is to see these people dance to these choreographed songs from different angles, repeated over and over, as if it costs them nothing."

If you go to the movies for vibrancy, contrast, character development, and aesthetics, the movie may not be for you. If you go to the movies for fun, stories, great acting, and to be captivated into a man's world whose story has never been captured more entertainingly — this is the movie. Go watch it.

9/10
The Bear's Verdict
A god amongst men, imperfectly but brilliantly told.

Jaafar Jackson is a genuine star. Colman Domingo is the villain you need. The film earns its spectacle — not through cynicism, but through a story too big to be cynical about. It misses the genius of the music-making, but captures everything else with enough force to make you forget what you came in knowing.
