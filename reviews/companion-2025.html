Film Review · 2025

Companion

Sci-Fi Thriller Sophie Thatcher · Jack Quaid 7 / 10
Listen Along Companion (2025) — Bear Reads the Review
0:00 --:--

It's a film obsessed with the gap between what we perform and what we feel — and it uses its genre framework not as a crutch but as a scalpel. The trouble is, it keeps putting the scalpel down to be a butter knife instead.

This movie tries to cross over the line between fun and serious far too much. They attempt in the beginning to have the characters seem one dimensional — all except the robot — and include shots with suspense like the dark outside of the secluded cabin when all the friends are dancing together. Nonetheless it is a fun ride, with excellent actors playing in these boxes the writers have etched for them to abide in.

You see glimpses of Sophia Thatcher's brilliance and robotic aura; both offputting and utterly mesmerising. One must feel for Jack Quaid who so constantly plays the innocent buffoon, but he does a great job with the lines and impressions. The rest of the cast does a good job, but I felt the entire time as if something was missing.

"Whether it was a truly shocking moment before the climax, or a broad statement about society amidst the sea of opportunity to do so — it dances back and forth between serious and unserious and never quite commits to either."

The plot twists are excellent in isolation, but collectively they make the story feel disjointed and outrageously unrealistic — yes, I see the hypocrisy of this statement amidst a story about AI robots, but we don't seem far off it in the real world. The movie is literally about people creating robots for their own pleasure, and somehow never fully commits to saying anything meaningful about that. The opportunity sits there, untouched, for the entire runtime.

All in all it felt kiddy and unserious, but fun and rambunctious. The stylistic energy reminds me of Send Help — although the humour in that made it far more entertaining. Maybe that's exactly what Companion missed: a good laugh in a very serious plot that decided to be unserious yet gory. That tonal split never resolves, and it leaves you watching something enjoyable but slightly hollow.

"Sophie Thatcher carries this film on her back through sheer presence alone. She deserves better material, and if this is what she can do with what she's given — watch her."

If you haven't seen it and have been wanting to, go watch it. If you haven't heard of it and sci-fi doesn't intrigue you, don't. It earns its fun even when it fumbles its ambitions — and there's enough here to keep you engaged from start to finish. Just don't expect it to linger.

7/10
The Bear's Verdict
Fun, hollow, and a little frustrating — in that order.

Companion is the film that could have been sharp and settles for being enjoyable. Sophie Thatcher is its saving grace and Jack Quaid is its reliable anchor, but the script keeps undercutting both. Worth your evening, not your deep analysis. Put it on, enjoy it, move on.
